Comparison of Potential and NLFFF Extrapolation Results for 64x64x64, 96x96x96, and 128x128x128 Dimensions

I’ve compared the results of potential and NLFFF (Nonlinear Force-Free Field) extrapolations for data cubes with dimensions of 64x64x64, 96x96x96, and 128x128x128. The z-slices of these 3D cubes at various heights are shown in the Jupyter notebook: test_compare_b3d.ipynb.

Key Details:

	•	The z-slice plots are provided for z=0, z=1, z=3, z=5, z=10, z=30, and z=60. For the larger data cubes (96x96x96 and 128x128x128), the corresponding z indices are scaled as z*96/64 and z*128/64, respectively.
	•	You can access the data cubes via this link: Google Drive. Download the three .h5 files and place them in the folder defined by the datapath variable in the notebook. You can then run the notebook on your local machine.

Summary of Results:

	1.	The potential field results for the 64x64x64, 96x96x96, and 128x128x128 data cubes are nearly identical.
	2.	The NLFFF results for the 96x96x96 and 128x128x128 cubes exhibit nonlinear force-free characteristics, whereas the 64x64x64 NLFFF result closely resembles the potential field result.
	3.	Artifacts are observed in the NLFFF results for the 96x96x96 and 128x128x128 data cubes, particularly around regions with strong magnetic fields. A zoom-in of these artifacts is provided in the notebook. These artifacts are more prominent in regions below a height of 20 (in the 64x64x64 scale, equivalent to 40 in the 128x128x128 scale).

Performance Issue:

As mentioned previously, the NLFFF code takes significantly longer to complete for larger data cube dimensions. In some cases, the extrapolation does not complete even after an overnight run on a Mac with an Intel chip.

I conducted additional tests using the same NLFFF code on both an Intel-based Mac and an Apple Silicon Mac, with identical Python environments (Python 3.10.13). On the Apple Silicon Mac, the code completes the extrapolation within 10 seconds, whereas on the Intel Mac, it runs much slower. I would appreciate assistance in understanding the performance discrepancy between the two systems.
